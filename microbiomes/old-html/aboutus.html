WHAT ARE MICROBIOMES







Microbiomes in humans

Germs are everywhere. And we, as humans, have come up with ways to remove them from our day to day lives, like washing our hands or using hand sanitizer. But it turns out that there are germs, also called microbes, living throughout our whole body and have developed habitance in our bodies. The places that these microbes and bacteria live is called a microbiome. There are fuzzy lines as to how many microbiomes there are in humans, but the most common number is 5: oral, skin, vaginal, nose, and, most popular, gut. [1] Over the last 15 years of research, scientists have found unthinkable connections between human microbiomes and physiological health. Microbiome research is very dependent on computational capabilities, hence why microbiomes were initially discovered in the late 1800s but not able to be analyzed until more recently. [2] To date, a lot has been discovered about microbiomes, but there is still much more to be uncovered.

Microbiomes elsewhere

In addition to being in humans and other mammals, microbiomes are known to exist in other ecosystems. Researchers have taken samples of soil to analyize their microbiome and found connections between it and the effects of climate change. [3] Understanding the connection and the ways that the microbes in the soil change and act can shine more light on the issues of climate change and potentially pose new ways to go about the issue.
